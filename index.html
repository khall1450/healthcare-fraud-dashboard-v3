An estimated 3% to 10% of total U.S. health spending is lost to health care fraud every year. This dashboard tracks the federal response starting in 2025, including criminal and civil enforcement actions and federal oversight and accountability measures. It also highlights investigative media coverage exposing fraud across the system.

Federal criminal and civil actions against health care fraud

Agencies: Primary agency is DOJ (the prosecutor). HHS-OIG is tagged when named as investigator or credited for assistance. Other federal investigators (FBI, DEA, FDA OCI, IRS-CI, HSI, USPIS, DCIS) and state Medicaid Fraud Control Units are not tagged individually.
Amounts: Aim to reflect size of the fraud — stated loss or scheme size when the press release names one, else the resolution amount. FCA settlements/judgments include statutory multipliers (up to 3× government loss plus per-claim penalties), so those typically exceed the underlying loss by 1.5×–3×. Ability-to-pay-adjusted criminal penalties are excluded. Read more

Where This Data Comes From
Federal Enforcement Actions tab

Every item on this tab has an action type of Criminal Enforcement or Civil Action and links to an official .gov press release. Content is auto-scraped daily from two sources, both DOJ:

  • DOJ Office of Public Affairs press feed — major takedowns, FCA settlements, and headline prosecutions.
  • DOJ U.S. Attorney's Offices — a consolidated scraper that paginates through the nationwide USAO press-release feed, surfacing items from any of the 93 federal districts.

Both DOJ scrapers use the same inclusion rule: if DOJ tagged the release with the "Health Care Fraud" topic tag, the item is included unconditionally. Items DOJ didn't tag pass only if all three of the following hold — the title contains a health-care-specific term, the body contains a fraud/enforcement keyword, and the body contains health care context.

The HHS-OIG press releases scraper also feeds this tab indirectly: OIG regularly announces enforcement outcomes by linking out to the DOJ press release. When an OIG item's link points to justice.gov or its title contains enforcement keywords (sentenced, indicted, convicted, agrees to pay, settlement, etc.), the ingestion code rewrites the agency field from HHS-OIG to DOJ and the item lands here instead of on Oversight.

A second gate ensures purity: if any Criminal Enforcement or Civil Action item comes through with an agency other than DOJ after all re-labeling, the ingestion code reclassifies its type to Audit so it routes to Oversight instead. The result is that 100% of items on the Federal Enforcement tab are DOJ.

Federal Oversight & Accountability tab

This tab holds every non-enforcement action type — audits, evaluations, investigations, rules, hearings, reports, administrative actions, and similar. Auto-scraped sources:

  • HHS-OIG — audit and evaluation reports feed, plus the subset of HHS-OIG press releases that aren't rewritten to DOJ by the enforcement re-routing described above (new audit announcements, industry advisories, oversight initiatives).
  • CMS — the CMS newsroom and the dedicated /fraud page.
  • GAO — the Government Accountability Office reports RSS feed.
  • Congress — committee-page scrapers for House Oversight, House Energy & Commerce, House Ways & Means, House Judiciary, Senate Finance, Senate HELP, and Senate Judiciary.
  • Congress.gov hearings — a separate daily job that pulls congressional hearing records via the Congress.gov API, keeps items whose title matches a health-care-fraud phrase list (Medicare/Medicaid/TRICARE/etc. + fraud/kickback/anti-kickback/false claims/pill mill/etc.) or whose committee is a health care committee with fraud-context title wording, and files the result as action type Hearing.
  • White House — whitehouse.gov press releases, fact sheets, and executive orders.
  • Treasury / FinCEN — FinCEN financial-crime advisories and enforcement actions.
  • MACPAC and MedPAC — congressional advisory commission reports on Medicaid and Medicare program integrity.

The HHS press-room scraper exists but is disabled because the page is Akamai-bot-blocked (returns 403 even through Playwright). Fraud-relevant HHS items typically cross-post to CMS and get captured there.

A small number of items on this tab are added manually when a notable federal accountability action doesn't have an official .gov press release — for example, a cabinet official's televised remarks announcing a crackdown, a task-force update covered only in the press, or a letter to state governors that never went out as an agency release.

Media Investigations tab

This tab has two parallel intake paths. In practice, the vast majority of items currently on the tab were added via the manual path.

Auto-scrape pipeline (runs daily).

A Google News RSS scraper runs a fixed set of queries (e.g. "medicare fraud" investigation, hospice fraud investigation, site:propublica.org healthcare fraud), filters candidates against a hard outlet whitelist (ProPublica, KFF Health News, STAT News, The New York Times, The Wall Street Journal, The Washington Post, CBS News, NPR, Bloomberg, Reuters, The Atlantic, The New Yorker, Los Angeles Times, Chicago Tribune, Boston Globe, Miami Herald, Tampa Bay Times, and the San Francisco Chronicle), then runs each surviving candidate through a Claude Haiku classifier whose prompt explicitly rejects agency-led stories, single-case press-release rewrites, hearing rewrites, opinion pieces, drug trafficking cases without a billing-fraud angle, and general healthcare policy coverage. Items that clear all three stages are committed to the tab without manual review.

Manual adds.

The author adds notable investigative-journalism stories directly, from any outlet whose reporting clearly fits the tab's scope — including outlets not on the auto-scrape whitelist. This path is how most items on the tab were added.

How items are classified

Tags, state, amount, and action type are extracted from the source text using a combination of regex rules and AI extraction with anchored citations (the model must quote the source verbatim for any field it populates, and the code validates that the quote actually appears in the article). Titles on the Federal Enforcement tab match the official press-release headline verbatim.

This dashboard does not generate original reporting. It aggregates publicly available federal enforcement and oversight activity into a single filterable view and links back to the original source for every item.

Accuracy and corrections

Classifications, tags, and dollar amounts on this dashboard are generated from a mix of automated extraction and manual curation, and errors are possible. The linked primary source is always the authoritative record. Please verify any specific figure, charge, or agency attribution against the underlying press release or report before citing it.
